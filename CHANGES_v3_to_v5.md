# ONA Synthetic Benchmark — v5

An interactive, browser-native tool for generating and visualising synthetic Organisational Network Analysis (ONA) benchmark datasets. Open `ona\_benchmark\_v5.html` in any modern browser. No install, no server, no dependencies.

## A Note on Realism and Applicability

The networks generated by this tool are synthetic by design. They are constructed from statistical models and calibrated parameters, not from observed relational data.

**Topological plausibility, not empirical accuracy.** The topology families (Erdős–Rényi, Watts–Strogatz, Barabási–Albert, SBM, Hierarchical Corporate) produce networks with properties that resemble real organisational networks in specific respects — clustering, hierarchy, hub formation — but any given generated network may not reflect how a real organisation of that size and industry actually operates.

**Synthetic outcomes are benchmarks, not predictions.** The `engagement\_score`, `innovation\_score`, `burnout\_risk`, and scenario-specific outcome variables are derived from structural position using plausible but simplified assumptions. They are suitable for testing dashboard rendering, method validation, and algorithm benchmarking — not for drawing conclusions about real people or real organisations.

**Industry templates are defaults, not ground truth.** The department mixes, seniority distributions, and location weights in the four industry templates are reasonable approximations, not validated empirical distributions.

**Scenario realism depends on parameter calibration.** The scenarios are tuned to produce structurally interesting networks for specific business questions. Whether any given scenario accurately represents the dynamics of a real reorganisation, merger, or AI adoption programme is a matter of expert judgement, not a guarantee of the tool.

As the original protocol author stated:

> \*"v3 is a major expansion of the benchmark protocol, but it is still a benchmark system rather than a complete digital twin of organisational life. \[...] synthetic outcomes are designed for benchmarking, not causal inference."\*
> — Fierăscu, S.I., ONA Synthetic Benchmark Protocol v3, Limitations

## What Is ONA?

Organisational Network Analysis maps how work actually happens inside a company — who collaborates, who seeks advice, who trusts whom, where silos form, where brokers carry too much load, and how change moves through the organisation. Unlike org-chart analysis, ONA works on the informal relational structure that drives productivity, innovation, and resilience.

Synthetic benchmark datasets allow researchers, practitioners, and product teams to:

* test and compare ONA algorithms on controlled, reproducible ground truth
* demonstrate ONA tooling without using real employee data (privacy-safe)
* model specific business questions before collecting real survey data
* validate platform outputs against known structural properties

## Quick Start

1. Download `ona\_benchmark\_v5.html`
2. Open it in Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge
3. Choose a scenario from the left panel
4. Adjust node count, seeds, and active layers as needed
5. Click **Generate**

No internet connection is required after the file is saved locally.

## Relational Layers

|Layer|Type|What it captures|
|-|-|-|
|`communication`|Undirected|Day-to-day information exchange|
|`trust`|Undirected|Psychological safety and reliance|
|`collaboration`|Undirected|Joint work on tasks and projects|
|`innovation`|Undirected|Idea-sharing and creative exchange|
|`energy`|Undirected|Who energises or drains whom|
|`advice`|Directed (seeker → advisor)|Who seeks expertise from whom|
|`reporting`|Directed (manager → report)|Formal management line|
|`mentorship`|Directed (mentor → mentee)|Developmental guidance relationships|
|`decision\_influence`|Directed (senior → junior)|Who shapes decisions|
|`tool\_interaction`|Directed|Human-to-AI-agent workflows|

Edge weights are context-aware: same-department, same-location, and same-legacy-company pairs receive calibrated weight adjustments per layer. From v5, `trust` and `mentorship` are generated by independent sparse graph models rather than being derived from the base topology.

## Changes from v3

v5 incorporates all changes introduced in v4, plus a second round of improvements focused on scenario-level plausibility — ensuring each preset generates a network whose structural signal is findable, interpretable, and consistent with what the scenario claims to show.

\---

### Changes introduced in v4

#### BA topology — power-law degree distribution

**v3 problem:** The preferential attachment inner loop recomputed the full weight sum on every candidate trial, O(n²) per arriving node. With `m=3` and power 1.2, the degree distribution was nearly Poisson — no visible fat tail.

**Fix:** Stochastic acceptance sampler, O(1) per trial. `m=5`, power 1.3, zero appeal 1.0. Seniority fitness multipliers applied during construction (executive = 3.5×). Post-construction rewiring increased from 10% to 15%.

#### HierCorp topology — star hierarchy

**v3 problem:** Separate tree per department with no global CEO. Edge keys used `Math.min/max` which lost directionality. Informal within-dept ties used `0.18 × choose2(n)` — near-clique for large departments. Uniform span of 4–8 regardless of level.

**Fix:** Single global CEO. Level-dependent span-of-control (exec: 3–4, IC managers: 10–12). Informal ties capped at `min(√dept\_size × 2, 40)` with seniority-biased acceptance. Cross-dept leadership bridges added between level ≥ 4 nodes.

#### SBM topology — degree heterogeneity

**v3 problem:** Flat block probability produced Poisson degree distribution within blocks.

**Fix:** Degree-corrected SBM. Per-node sociability `θ\_i` from Pareto(α=1.5) × seniority multiplier. Connection probability: `p\_ij = block\[bi]\[bj] × θ\_i × θ\_j`, normalised per block.

#### Layer derivation — hub structure preservation

**v3 problem:** Uniform keep-probability per edge destroyed the heavy tail of the base graph in every derived layer.

**Fix:** Keep-probability scales with `max(degree\_i, degree\_j) / max\_degree` via a layer-specific hub-boost multiplier (communication 0.60, mentorship 0.20). High-degree nodes retain centrality in all derived layers.

#### DEMO\_M silo calibration

**v3 problem:** Between-block probability 0.005 produced two near-disconnected cliques, not a nuanced silo.

**Fix:** Between-block raised to 0.02. E-I index approximately −0.25 to −0.35.

#### Validation

Added Degree Variance-to-Mean Ratio (VMR > 1.5) check. Confirms non-Poisson degree distribution — exposes flat distributions that passed all other v3 checks.

#### Visual design

Migrated from dark-mode neon palette to Slack Pulse warm parchment light-mode. Primary accent: indigo `#4f46e5`. Brand wordmark: Fraunces serif. Attribution link added.

### Changes introduced in v5

#### Node data — tenure and hire cohort are now consistent

**v3/v4 problem:** `tenure\_years` was drawn from an independent lognormal distribution while `hire\_cohort` was sampled from a weighted year list. The two attributes were statistically uncorrelated: a person hired in 2024 might have 9 years of tenure; a 2017 hire might have 0.3 years. Anyone glancing at the raw CSV would immediately notice the inconsistency.

**Fix:** `tenure\_years` is now derived from `hire\_cohort`: `tenure = (2026 − cohort) × U(0.6, 1.0) − small\_jitter`, clipped to a plausible range. A 2017 hire has 7–9 years tenure; a 2026 hire has 0.1–0.5 years. The two attributes are now internally consistent.

#### Gender encoding

**v3/v4 problem:** Gender was binary (`female`/`male`) with a hardcoded 46/54 split.

**Fix:** Three values — `female` (46%), `male` (50%), `non-binary` (4%). Split is configurable via weights.

#### Trust and mentorship — independent layer generation

**v3/v4 problem:** All relational layers were derived by randomly subsampling edges from the base topology. This meant trust and mentorship ties could only exist between pairs that were already connected in the base graph. A quiet communicator — low degree in the communication layer — could not have any trust ties, even though deep trust relationships are empirically independent of communication frequency.

**Fix:** `trust` and `mentorship` are now generated by independent sparse graph models that do not consult the base topology.

* **Trust:** Independent generator targeting average degree \~2.5. Acceptance probability incorporates same-department (+0.25), same-location (+0.10), and level-similarity (+0.15 for same level, −0.08 for gap ≥ 3). Even peripheral communicators can have strong trust ties.
* **Mentorship:** Independent generator; only level ≥ 4 nodes can be mentors. \~40% of ICs and managers receive a mentor. 30% of mentorship ties cross department boundaries. Average degree \~1.2 — appropriately sparse.

#### Advice edge direction — corrected

**v3/v4 problem:** `advice` edges were directed from higher-level to lower-level nodes (senior → junior). This is the wrong semantic — that is mentorship, not advice-seeking. In advice networks, the convention is that the edge points from the seeker toward the person being consulted.

**Fix:** `advice` edges now point from seeker (junior) to advisor (senior). All directed layers now carry explicit comments documenting their convention: `reporting` and `mentorship` point senior → junior; `decision\_influence` points senior → junior; `advice` points junior → senior.

#### REORG\_M — before/after structural signal

**v3/v4 problem:** REORG used `hier\_corp` topology but the reorg signal existed only in the topology itself. There were no before/after attributes, and no edge-level signal of the structural change.

**Fix:** Every node now carries `team\_before` (original department) and `team\_after` (new cross-functional squad, 4–6 squads mixing 2–3 departments each). Collaboration and trust edges that cross the new squad boundary are thinned by 55% in `applyScenarioEdgeModifiers`, representing the collaboration debt that accumulates after a restructure. New outcome variable: `transition\_stress` (burnout × low change\_readiness × cross-squad displacement).

#### RESIZE\_M — deliberately narrow spans

**v3/v4 problem:** Span-of-control in `hier\_corp` was calibrated for realism (managers with 8–12 reports). For a delayering scenario, this means the hierarchy looked reasonable — the opposite of what the scenario is supposed to show.

**Fix:** RESIZE\_M uses narrow span overrides (level 6: 2 spans, level 5: 2, level 4: 3, level 3: 4). This produces 6–7 hierarchy levels in a 500-node network. Nodes now carry `span\_of\_control` (direct reports) and `hierarchy\_depth` (distance from CEO root) attributes, filled from the actual constructed tree. `at\_risk\_role` extended to include Quality department. New outcome: `overload\_risk` (burnout × span-normalised load).

#### SUCCESSION\_M — key person risk from betweenness

**v3/v4 problem:** `key\_person\_candidate` was flagged for nodes with high seniority *or* high AI adoption — a logically inconsistent combination with no connection to succession risk.

**Fix:** `is\_key\_person` flags nodes at or above the 80th percentile of `level`. `key\_person\_risk\_score` is computed after betweenness is available, combining level (35%), betweenness (45%), and performance (20%). `successor\_readiness` is a composite of performance × change\_readiness × seniority proximity. Both attributes are exported in the node CSV and payload.

#### CULTURE\_M — siloed baseline contrast

**v3/v4 problem:** The SBM between-block probability was raised to 0.08 as a floor, meaning the network already had substantial cross-department ties before culture carrier edges were added. The community-of-practice finding had no structural contrast to stand against.

**Fix:** Between-block probability reduced to 0.015 (strong silo baseline). Culture carrier `energy` ties added on top of this sparse baseline create visible cross-boundary bridges. The contrast between the siloed communication layer and the cross-cutting energy layer is now the findable signal. Nodes are flagged `culture\_carrier` if `change\_readiness ≥ 75th percentile` or if they belong to a culture-forward department.

#### MA\_M — legacy split by department, not index order

**v3/v4 problem:** Legacy A/B was assigned to the first/second half of the node array, which was generated in department order. This meant Legacy A employees all came from the first half of the department list and Legacy B from the second half — the two companies had non-overlapping functional coverage, which is unrealistic for a merger.

**Fix:** Legacy assignment alternates by department index (even-indexed departments = Legacy A, odd = Legacy B). Both legacy companies now have representation across all major functions, matching the typical merger scenario where two full organisations combine.

#### Scenario descriptions updated

All ten scenario descriptions were rewritten to accurately reflect the structural signal each network contains, what attributes to look for, and what the expected finding is. The v3 descriptions referenced features (temporal snapshots, node-removal simulation) that were not implemented in the HTML tool.
